Javan Rhinoceros

The Javan Rhinoceros, a grey, single horned species, has the shocking status of being critically endangered. Only 60-63 Javan Rhinos exist today.


The Species

Rhinoceros Sondaicus, their given scientific name, belong to the Rhinocerotidae family, being the rarest member out of the 5. It has a horn that usually reaches 10 inches, and loose skin folds. The weights can range from about 2,000 to 5,000 pounds! Their height is from 5 to 5.5 feet tall and length, 6 to 11.5 feet wide. They typically habituate in tropical forests, and eat tree leaves, branches, and bark.

     

Critically Endangered

The shocking truth about these animals is the possibility of long-term survival is decreasing. There are currently 60-63 Javan Rhinos living in total, all located in Indonesia. Ujung Kulon National Park keeps the remaining animals in a tightly secure habitat. With such a small area to live in, they are at risk of inbreeding, causing problems in the species. The sanctuary also has a large Arenga palm tree that causes a decline in vegetation. Because of where the Javan Rhinos prefer to live, along the coastline, they are at a high risk with tsunamis and another natural disatsers. They are also faced with such a threat of extinction because of hunting for their horns. The last remaining Javan Rhino in Vietnam was killed and removed of it's horn from a poacher.

  What Can We Do?

World Wild Life Foundation has created a plan to save the population for the Javan species. Keeping all the animals in one habitat geographically can cause a decrease in reproduction, so they decided to spread the habitats to two locations. This new sanctuary is located in a place outside of potential natural disasters, and will benefit breeding. We all can help this plan move to action by donating.
